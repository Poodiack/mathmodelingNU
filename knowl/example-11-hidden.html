A shepherd wants to predict the volume of wool he will get from a sheep, \(V\text{,}\) in terms of the girth of the sheep (the distance around the fattest part of the belly). The volume is the thickness of the wool times the area from which it is shaved. This area is not a simple shape, so we will use geometric similarity to simplify the model. Consider the following assumptions:

  1. The thickness of the wool is the same for every sheep.

  2. The area on each sheep from which the wool is shaved is geometrically similar.

The first assumption allows us to model

\begin{equation*} V\propto A \end{equation*}

where \(A\) is the area from which the wool is shaved. The second assumption allows us to model

\begin{equation*} A\propto \ell^2 \end{equation*}

where \(\ell\) is some characteristic dimension. We will choose the girth (the distance around the belly of the sheep) to be this dimension. Combining the two proportionalities using transitivity, we get

\begin{equation*} V\propto \ell^2 \end{equation*}

To find the constant of proportionality, and test the assumptions, we would need to collect data of volume and girth.
